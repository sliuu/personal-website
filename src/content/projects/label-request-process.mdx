## Project Brief

Human-labeled data is central to powering the self-driving infrastructure at Waymo, to turn what the car sees into data models can understand. To this end, we might label data with anything from 2D Bounding Boxes to timestamp markers, to answering general questions about a run. When new types of labels are required, ML Engineers need to clearly communicate to Labeling Ops how the data should be labeled, and the exact criteria.

Historically, the labeling team would handhold ML engineers in making these custom labeling requests at the beginning until they could do it themselves. But as the company grew, it became unrealistic to require labeling team engineers any time a new labeling request was needed, and for the documentation to be obtuse and outdated.

## Key Insights

Visualizing the current process in a flow chart (below) allowed me to map out the many entry points users resporting using to start a labeling request. 

<figure>
<img src="/projects/label-request-process-flow.png" alt="Flow chart of the labeling request process" class="hero"/>
</figure>

From there, I tracked all the information that was ultimately needed to make a labeling request, and chose better groupings for related information. I also learned what settings users commonly changed, and what advanced settings they rarely use, to avoid creating information overwhelm for users.

I also noticed that users didn't understand all the terms and had a hard time figuring out what things meant. I needed to find a way to make screens like the one below less intimidating for users, and make documentation more clear.

<figure>
<img src="/projects/labeling-requesting-old.png" alt="Image of old label requesting page" class="hero"/>
</figure>

## The Solution

I decided to use a Wizard pattern for the labeling request process. I grouped related settings together, made documentation clear instead of hiding it in tooltips, and hid advanced settings to not overwhelm users.
